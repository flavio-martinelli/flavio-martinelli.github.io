## Prelude

Why do neural networks need to be so large to train well? A popular explanation uses the metaphor of lottery tickets:

<div style="text-align: center; margin: 1rem 10rem;">
<em>"If you want to win the lottery, just buy a lot of tickets and some will likely win.<br>Buying a lot of tickets = having an overparameterized neural network for your task </em><span class="citation-hover" style="color: var(--global-theme-color);"><sup style="font-weight: 700;">1</sup><span class="citation-tooltip">Slides from <a href="https://www.cs.princeton.edu/courses/archive/spring21/cos598D/lectures/pruning.pdf">Princeton CS-598D course</a></span></span>."
</div>

where a ticket is intended to be a subnetwork together with its initialization. Given that there exist subnetworks that can be trained successfully in isolation<span class="citation-hover" style="color: var(--global-theme-color);"><sup style="font-weight: 700;">2</sup><span class="citation-tooltip"><a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.03635">Frankle & Carbin, 2019</a></span></span>, this metaphor gained popularity to explain the need for overparameterized models: large networks are needed **because** they contain more potential winning subnetworks (tickets). Even Wikipedia states:

<div style="text-align: center; margin: 1rem 8rem;">
<em>"the chance of any given ticket winning is tiny, but if you buy enough of them you are certain to win, and the number of possible subnetworks increases exponentially as the <strong>power set</strong> of the set of connections, making the number of possible subnetworks astronomical for any reasonably large network </em><span class="citation-hover" style="color: var(--global-theme-color);"><sup style="font-weight: 700;">3</sup><span class="citation-tooltip">Wikipedia: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lottery_ticket_hypothesis#:~:text=The%20term%20derived,large%20network.[2]">"Lottery ticket hypothesis"</a></span></span>."
</div>

We show that part of the community interprets this metaphor too literally, leading to wrong intuitions on the mechanisms of optimization in deep neural networks; in particular, we see no evidence of these combinatorially fast scaling laws.
So we wrote an opinion piece proposing an alternative intuition, grounded in loss landscape theory.

Without invoking subnetworks or having to resolve their interactions, we propose a new mental picture: **Escape Dimensions**.<br>
In short, escape dimensions are new dimensions that are added to the optimization landscape when we make our networks wider. These new dimensions serve as escape routes for gradient descent, to avoid getting trapped into high-loss, bad, local minima.

We collect relevant theoretical and empirical results on loss landscapes under a new, intuitive lens called: <a href="https://infoscience.epfl.ch/handle/20.500.14299/263577" style="color: var(--global-theme-color);"><span style="font-weight: 700; color: inherit;">Escape Dimensions Theory.</span></a>
